This page reads across everything the project collects — deprivation, civic fabric, energy knowledge bases, grid headroom, community energy itself and Energy Redress funding — with one question in mind: if you wanted community energy to grow where it would do the most good, where would you start, and what would have to change? 1Community energy mostly is not where deprivation is

2The gap is upstream, in the conditions

It would be easy to read the pattern above as deprived places being unable or unwilling to do community energy. The data points somewhere else. Presence tilts away from deprivation only moderately (r = – across authorities), but the enabling conditions — civic fabric, knowledge nearby, grid headroom — tilt away twice as hard (r = –). And once you hold those conditions constant, deprivation stops predicting presence almost entirely (partial correlation –).

Deprived places are not worse at turning conditions into community energy. They have fewer conditions to turn.

The typology makes the same point visually. Just over half of the most deprived fifth of authorities sit in the Cold quadrant — thin conditions, little community energy — against – of the least deprived fifth. But look at the Pioneering band: community energy that exists despite thin conditions is disproportionately a deprived-area phenomenon (– of the most deprived fifth, versus – of the least). Where the sector does take root in hard ground, that ground is usually deprived — which is the opposite of what a “deprived places can’t” story would predict.

The practical implication: effort aimed at deprived areas should go into the conditions themselves — paid local organising capacity where volunteer civic fabric is thin, rather than competitions that reward places already organised enough to bid. Exhortation aimed at communities, without that, asks them to convert assets they don't have.

4It can clearly work in deprived places

The strongest argument against fatalism is already in the data. These are the authorities in the most deprived third with the strongest community-energy presence per head — places that falsify the idea that deprivation itself is the barrier:

Plymouth tops the list by a wide margin, and its story is instructive: Plymouth Energy Community was founded with the city council's active involvement, an anchor institution doing the early heavy lifting that volunteer civic fabric would otherwise have to supply. The pattern that deserves proper investigation — this dataset can pose the question but not answer it — is how many of the other pioneers share that shape: a council, housing association or similar body lowering the start-up bar. If most do, that is the replicable mechanism for the cold quadrant.

5One funding stream already follows need — build on it

Energy Redress money (Ofgem fines, redistributed to energy-hardship projects) is the counter-example to everything above: it tracks deprivation well. The most deprived two fifths of authorities receive – per person against – in the least deprived two fifths.

That matters for growth strategy because it means trusted, energy-literate delivery organisations are already funded and working in deprived places that have no community-energy organisation of their own — – authorities in the most deprived third fit that description. These are footholds: the relationships and credibility that a new community energy organisation spends its first years building already exist there, attached to fuel-poverty work rather than generation or ownership. Connecting that advice infrastructure to asset ownership is a much shorter bridge than starting cold.

6A null result worth knowing: proximity to expertise predicts nothing

Distance to the nearest energy knowledge base — universities with energy research, catapults, DNO headquarters and the like — has essentially no relationship with community-energy presence (r = –). Newham is five kilometres from Ofgem's front door and has no community energy at all; Manchester, with one of the country's largest university energy programmes in the middle of it, has plenty. Read with the previous sections, this suggests expertise travels — what does not travel is organising capacity. Money spent putting knowledge near places is probably worth less than money spent putting organisers in them. (The measure is crude — straight-line distance to an institution says nothing about actual relationships — so treat this as a prompt for better evidence, not a verdict.)

7Where to start, in three tiers

Putting the threads together — need, conditions, presence, and what Redress already knows — three different kinds of place call for three different kinds of effort.

The first tier is where conventional support (grant rounds, technical assistance, shared-ownership offers) should convert fastest: the conditions exist, the sector just has not arrived. The second tier is where the cheapest route in is through organisations already funded and trusted. The third tier — – authorities, – million people, almost all deep in the cold quadrant — is where nothing short of patient, paid capacity-building will move the needle, and where competitive funding designed around shovel-ready bids will keep not arriving.

Caveats, before anyone quotes this

  • Organisation locations are registered offices — where work happens can differ, and city-centre registered offices can sit in deprived LSOAs for unrelated reasons. Redress projects are placed at the local authority or town named for the project where known (about three in five), else the grantee's registered office.
  • Capacity comes from the community-maintained CEE map (2024 vintage); many schemes record no size, so totals are floors and single large schemes dominate some areas.
  • Grid headroom covers only the Northern Powergrid and SSEN SEPD licence areas; elsewhere readiness uses a neutral grid score.
  • Everything here is cross-sectional association. None of it establishes what causes what — the pioneers section in particular is a research question, not a finding.
  • Deprivation is the 2025 English Indices, population-weighted to authorities. Method detail: the data explorer and the repository.
