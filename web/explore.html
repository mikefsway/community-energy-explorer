Every English local authority scored on three axes — need (deprivation), readiness (civic fabric, energy knowledge nearby, grid headroom) and presence (community energy already on the ground). Use it to find where the sector is thriving, where it could most easily grow, and where it would struggle.

How the explorer is built

Every metric is rolled up to the local authority district (296 in England). The three headline axes are percentile ranks across those authorities:

  • Need — population-weighted deprivation (English Indices of Deprivation 2025). Higher = more deprived.
  • Readiness — the enabling conditions, deliberately excluding existing community energy so the comparison is meaningful: civic fabric (charities + community venues per resident, 50%), proximity to an energy knowledge base (25%), and green grid headroom where the network operator publishes it (25%).
  • Presence — community energy already on the ground: registered societies, Community Energy England members and CEE-map project sites, per 100,000 people. Ranked as the share of authorities strictly below, so an authority with no community energy at all scores exactly 0.

From these:

  • Opportunity = readiness − presence. High means good conditions but little community energy yet — the easiest wins.
  • Struggle = need × (1 − readiness). High means deprived and thin on enabling conditions — the hardest ground.
  • Typology — a median split of readiness × presence into thriving, latent (easy win), pioneering and cold.

Caveats: installed capacity comes from the community-maintained CEE map (2024 vintage) and not every scheme records a size, so capacity totals are a floor, not a census. Grid headroom covers only the Northern Powergrid and SSEN SEPD areas; elsewhere readiness uses a neutral grid score. Organisation and redress locations are registered offices or stated project towns, which may differ from where work actually happens. Full sources and method: the repository.
